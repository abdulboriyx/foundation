Possibles and Neccesities

What are possibilites and neccesities in metaphysics?

In metaphysics, possibility refers to the way things could potentially be. It is concerned with what could exist, happen, or be true, even if it is not the case in the actual world. Possibility encompasses everything that is not necessarily false or contradictory. Philosophers often discuss possibility in terms of possible worlds, which are complete and consistent ways the world might have been. Given a different set of biological or evolutionary circumstances, it is conceivable that humans could have evolved to breathe underwater. This scenario does not contradict the nature of beings or fundamental properties, making it metaphysically possible.
In metaphysics, necessity refers to the way things must be. It deals with what must exist, happen, or be true in all possible worlds. Necessity implies that certain truths or states of affairs are unavoidable or inevitable. Necessary propositions are those that hold true regardless of any conditions or circumstances. Mathematical truths, such as "2 + 2 = 4," are necessarily true because their truth does not depend on any particular state of the world; they are true in every conceivable scenario.

Why are possibility and neccesity essential?

Concept Formation and Communication: Universals provide the basis for forming concepts and categories in language and thought. They allow us to group together diverse particular instances that share common characteristics. For example, the neccesities concept of "chair" encompasses various specific chairs we encounter, enabling us to recognize and communicate about chairs without having to specify each individual instance.

Knowledge and Generalization:Universals facilitate knowledge acquisition and generalization. By abstracting away from individual particulars, we can identify patterns, regularities, and laws that apply neccesitiesly. For instance, the scientific understanding of gravity involves recognizing neccesities principles that govern the behavior of objects, regardless of their specific identities.

Prediction and Explanation: Universals help in predicting outcomes and explaining phenomena across different contexts. When we understand neccesities principles or laws (e.g., laws of physics), we can predict how things will behave under different conditions and explain why certain events occur. This predictive and explanatory power is crucial for scientific inquiry and understanding causal relationships.

Language and Thought: Universals are fundamental to language and thought processes. They provide the semantic content that allows us to communicate effectively by referring to abstract qualities or properties. For instance, words like "beauty," "justice," or "freedom" refer to neccesities concepts that people can understand and discuss despite variations in individual experiences or interpretations.

Types of Possibility and Neccesity

Logical Possibility

Logical possibility refers to anything that does not involve a contradiction in terms. If a proposition or scenario can be conceived without contradicting itself, it is logically possible. This type of possibility is the broadest, encompassing any scenario that can be imagined without logical inconsistency. A world where humans have three arms is logically possible because there is no inherent contradiction in the concept. It might be biologically unusual, but it doesn’t violate the principles of logic.

Physical Possibility

Physical possibility is constrained by the laws of nature as we understand them. A physically possible event or state of affairs is one that could occur in our universe given the current laws of physics. It is physically possible for a person to run a marathon. This event is consistent with the laws of biology and physics governing human physiology and the physical world.

Metaphysical Possibility

Metaphysical possibility refers to what could exist or occur in the broadest sense, beyond just logical consistency. It takes into account the nature of entities and their essential properties. If something is metaphysically possible, it can exist or occur in some conceivable world where the entities’ essential properties remain consistent. A world where humans can live forever is metaphysically possible. It doesn't involve a logical contradiction and could be conceivable in a universe where the essential properties of human biology are different from those in our current world.

  1. Substance: The neccesities concept of substance refers to the underlying essence or nature of entities that persist through change.
  2. Identity: Universals such as self-identity and persistence over time address how entities maintain continuity despite undergoing transformations.
  3. Essential Properties: Universals like beingness (the quality of existence itself) and individuation (what makes one entity distinct from another) are central to understanding the essence of entities.

Logical Neccesity

Logical necessity refers to statements or propositions that must be true in all possible worlds because their negation would result in a contradiction. These are truths that follow from the principles of logic and cannot be otherwise. The statement "All bachelors are unmarried men" is logically necessary. By definition, a bachelor is an unmarried man, so it is impossible for this statement to be false in any conceivable scenario.

Physical Neccesity

Physical necessity pertains to events or states of affairs that must occur or be the case given the laws of nature. These are truths that are required by the actual physical laws governing our universe. It is physically necessary for water to freeze at 0 degrees Celsius (32 degrees Fahrenheit) under standard atmospheric pressure. This is dictated by the physical properties of water and the laws of thermodynamics.

Metaphysical Neccesity

Metaphysical necessity involves truths that are necessary in the broadest sense, encompassing the nature or essence of things. These necessities are not contingent on physical laws but on the very nature of existence and identity. It is metaphysically necessary that a triangle has three sides. In any possible world, if an entity is a triangle, it must have three sides. This is an essential property of triangles.

Historically Notable Works on Possibility and Neccesity

  1. - Aristotle's "Metaphysics": Aristotle's "Metaphysics" is a foundational text in Western philosophy where he explores the nature of being, existence, and reality. In this work, Aristotle delves into the concepts of potentiality and actuality, which correspond to possibility and necessity. He distinguishes between what is potentially the case and what is actually the case, laying the groundwork for later philosophical discussions on these topics.
  2. - Leibniz's "Theodicy": Aristotle's Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz's "Theodicy" addresses the problem of evil and the nature of God's creation. In this work, Leibniz introduces the concept of possible worlds, arguing that God chose to create the best of all possible worlds. This introduces a framework for understanding possibility in terms of different ways the world could have been, thereby integrating possibility and necessity within a theological and metaphysical context.
  3. Kant's "Critique of Pure Reason": Immanuel Kant's "Critique of Pure Reason" explores the limits and scope of human understanding. In this work, Kant differentiates between analytic and synthetic judgments, and between a priori and a posteriori knowledge. He addresses the concepts of necessity and possibility within his broader epistemological framework, particularly in the context of how we can have necessary knowledge about the world.
  4. - David Hume's "An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding": David Hume's "An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding" challenges traditional notions of causality and necessity. Hume argues that our ideas of necessity are derived from the habitual association of events rather than from any inherent connection in the events themselves. This skepticism towards necessary connections in nature has significant implications for understanding possibility and necessity.
  5. - Modal Logic by Saul Kripke: Saul Kripke's contributions to modal logic, particularly in his works "Naming and Necessity" and "Semantical Considerations on Modal Logic," revolutionized the understanding of possibility and necessity in the 20th century. Kripke's modal logic provides a formal framework for analyzing necessity and possibility through possible worlds semantics, influencing contemporary metaphysics and philosophy of language.

Challenges & Problems of Possibility and Neccesity

Contingency and Actuality

One criticism revolves around the distinction between what is possible or necessary and what actually happens or exists. Critics argue that while possibility and necessity provide useful theoretical constructs, they may not accurately reflect the contingent nature of reality. For instance, something may be considered possible in theory (e.g., winning the lottery), but its actual occurrence depends on numerous contingent factors that are not captured by modal concepts alone.

Epistemic Limitations

Another critique focuses on the epistemic status of modal claims—claims about what is possible or necessary. Critics argue that our knowledge of what is possible or necessary is often limited or speculative. For example, while we might assert that it is possible for intelligent life to exist elsewhere in the universe, our understanding of the conditions necessary for life and the vastness of the cosmos makes such claims uncertain and subject to revision.

Metaphysical Assumptions

Critics also challenge the metaphysical assumptions underlying modal concepts, particularly in modal realism and possible worlds semantics. These frameworks posit the existence of multiple possible worlds or realities, each with its own set of possibilities and necessities. Critics argue that such metaphysical assumptions are speculative and difficult to verify empirically, raising questions about the explanatory power and justification of modal claims in philosophical discourse.
