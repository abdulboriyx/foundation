Key Terms in Philosophy

  1. A priori - Knowledge that is independent of experience, such as mathematical truths or logical deductions.
  2. A posteriori - Knowledge that is derived from experience or empirical evidence
  3. Metaphysics - The branch of philosophy that studies the nature of reality, existence, and the universe beyond the physical realm
  4. Epistemology - The study of knowledge, its nature, sources, limits, and validity
  5. Imperialism - A policy of extending a country's power and influence through diplomacy or military force. Example: European imperialism in Africa in the 19th century.
  6. Ethics - The branch of philosophy concerned with moral principles and how individuals ought to act
  7. Ontology - The study of being and existence, exploring what entities exist and how they can be grouped and related
  8. Logic - The study of principles of valid reasoning and argument.
  9. Empiricism - The belief that knowledge comes primarily from sensory experience.
  10. Dualism - The belief that reality consists of two distinct, often opposing, substances or principles, like mind and body.
  11. Rationalism - The view that reason is the primary source of knowledge, rather than experience.
  12. Monism - The belief that reality is composed of only one substance or principle, such as matter or spirit.
  13. Existentialism - A philosophy that emphasizes individual freedom, choice, and existence, often focusing on the meaning of life.
  14. Utalitarianism - An ethical theory that posits the best action is the one that maximizes overall happiness or utility.
  15. Deontology - An ethical theory that emphasizes duties and rules over consequences in determining right actions.
  16. Virtue Ethics - An ethical theory that focuses on developing good character traits (virtues) rather than specific rules for behavior.
  17. Causal Determinism - The belief that all events, including moral choices, are determined by previously existing causes.
  18. Free Will - The idea that individuals have the power to make choices and control their own actions.
  19. Nihilism - The belief that life is without objective meaning, purpose, or intrinsic value.
  20. Pragmatism - A philosophy that evaluates theories or beliefs based on their practical consequences or usefulness.
  21. Relativism - The view that truth and moral values are not absolute but are relative to the perspectives or cultures that hold them.
  22. Phenomenology - A philosophical movement focused on the structures of experience and consciousness.
  23. Solipsism - The belief that only one's own mind is certain to exist; extreme skepticism about the existence of anything outside one's own consciousness.
  24. Teleology - The study of purpose or design in natural phenomena, often concerned with the goals or ends of actions or objects.
  25. Subjectivism - The theory that perception and personal experiences are the primary sources of truth and meaning.
  26. Realism - The belief that external reality exists independently of human thought or perception.
  27. Idealism - The belief that reality is fundamentally mental or immaterial, and the physical world is a construction of the mind.
  28. Skepticism - A questioning attitude toward knowledge, facts, or opinions/beliefs stated as facts.
  29. Dialectic - A method of argument involving the resolution of contradictions, used to discover truth through logical reasoning.
  30. Categorical Imperative - Kant's ethical principle that one should act only according to maxims that could be universally applied.
  31. Eudaimonia - An ancient Greek concept of happiness or flourishing, often viewed as the ultimate goal of life in Aristotelian ethics.
  32. Tabula Rasa - The idea that individuals are born without built-in mental content, and all knowledge comes from experience or perception.
  33. Atomism - The theory that the universe and all matter are composed of small, indivisible particles called atoms.
  34. Determinism - The doctrine that all events, including human action, are ultimately determined by causes external to the will.
  35. Libertarianism (Free Will) - The belief that individuals possess free will and are not entirely determined by external forces or causes.
  36. Reductionism - The idea that complex phenomena can be explained by reducing them to their most basic components.
  37. Falsiability - The criterion introduced by Karl Popper that for a theory to be scientific, it must be capable of being proven false.
  38. Analytic Philosophy - A method of philosophical analysis that focuses on clarity of expression and logical argumentation, often through linguistic analysis.
  39. Continental Philosophy - A set of 19th- and 20th-century philosophical traditions from continental Europe, focusing on human experience, history, and society.
  40. Hedonism - The ethical theory that pleasure is the highest good and proper aim of human life.
  41. Essentialism - The belief that things have a set of characteristics or properties that make them what they are.
  42. Moral Relativism - The view that moral judgments are valid only relative to the culture or individual holding them, without objective standards.
  43. Moral Absolutism - The belief that certain actions are morally right or wrong regardless of context, consequences, or cultural beliefs.
  44. Alturism - The principle or practice of concern for the welfare of others, even at one's own expense.
  45. Humeâ€™s Guillotine - The argument that one cannot derive an "ought" from an "is," distinguishing between descriptive statements and prescriptive ethics.
  46. Panentheism - The belief that God is greater than the universe and exists both within and beyond it.
  47. Positivism - A philosophical theory advocating that knowledge is based on observable, scientific facts and laws, rather than metaphysical speculation.
  48. Theodicy - An attempt to explain why a benevolent and omnipotent God allows the existence of evil and suffering in the world.
  49. Materialism - The belief that reality is primarily composed of physical matter, and all phenomena, including consciousness, arise from material interactions.
  50. Existence Precedes Essence - An existentialist principle, particularly associated with Jean-Paul Sartre, which asserts that individuals create their own essence or nature through actions, rather than being defined by any inherent nature.
  51. Stoicism - An ancient Greek philosophy that teaches the development of self-control and fortitude as a means to overcome destructive emotions, focusing on virtue and wisdom as the path to a good life.
