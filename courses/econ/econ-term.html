Key Terms in Economics

Key Terms in Economics

  1. Supply and Demand - A fundamental economic model describing how the quantity of goods and services supplied by producers and demanded by consumers interact to determine prices and quantities in a market.
  2. Gross Domestic Product (GDP) - The total value of all goods and services produced within a country over a specific period, used as an indicator of economic health and growth.
  3. Inflation - The rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services is rising, eroding purchasing power.
  4. Opportunity Cost - The value of the next best alternative foregone when a decision is made to pursue a particular option.
  5. Marginal Utility - TThe additional satisfaction or benefit derived from consuming one more unit of a good or service.
  6. Elasticity - A measure of how much the quantity demanded or supplied of a good responds to changes in price or income.
  7. Monetary Policy - The process by which a central bank manages the supply of money and interest rates to achieve macroeconomic objectives such as controlling inflation and fostering economic growth.
  8. Fiscal Policy - Government adjustments to spending and taxation policies to influence economic activity and stabilize the economy.
  9. Market Equilibrium - The state in which the quantity demanded of a good or service equals the quantity supplied, resulting in a stable price.
  10. Perfect Competition - A market structure characterized by many buyers and sellers, homogeneous products, and no barriers to entry or exit.
  11. Monopoly - A market structure where a single firm controls the entire market for a good or service, with significant barriers to entry for other firms.
  12. Oligopoly - A market structure dominated by a few large firms, each of which has significant market power and the ability to influence prices.
  13. Monopolistic Competition - A market structure with many firms offering differentiated products, allowing for some degree of market power and pricing flexibility.
  14. Demand Curve - A graphical representation of the relationship between the price of a good and the quantity demanded by consumers.
  15. Supply Curve - A graphical representation of the relationship between the price of a good and the quantity supplied by producers.
  16. Price Elasticity of Demand (PED) - A measure of how responsive the quantity demanded of a good is to a change in its price.
  17. Price Elasticity of Supply - A measure of how responsive the quantity supplied of a good is to a change in its price.
  18. Income Elasticity of Demand - A measure of how the quantity demanded of a good changes in response to a change in consumer income.
  19. Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand - A measure of how the quantity demanded of one good responds to a change in the price of another good.
  20. Economic Growth - The increase in the output of goods and services in an economy over time, typically measured by the growth rate of GDP.
  21. Recession - A period of economic decline characterized by decreasing GDP, high unemployment, and reduced consumer spending.
  22. Depression - A prolonged and severe downturn in economic activity, more intense than a recession, often involving significant declines in GDP and high levels of unemployment.
  23. Stagflation - An economic condition characterized by stagnant growth, high unemployment, and high inflation.
  24. Business Cycle - The natural rise and fall of economic growth that occurs over time, including phases such as expansion, peak, contraction, and trough.
  25. Interest Rate - The cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage of the principal amount borrowed.
  26. Exchange Rate - The rate at which one currency can be exchanged for another currency.
  27. Trade Balance - The difference between the value of a country's exports and imports of goods and services.
  28. Fiscal Deficit - The amount by which a government's expenditures exceed its revenues, leading to borrowing needs.
  29. National Debt - The total amount of money that a government owes to creditors, accumulated over time through fiscal deficits.
  30. Externalities - Costs or benefits of economic activities that affect third parties who are not directly involved in the transaction.
  31. Public Goods - Goods that are non-excludable and non-rivalrous, meaning that they can be consumed by many individuals simultaneously without diminishing their availability to others.
  32. Private Goods - Goods that are both excludable and rivalrous, meaning that consumption by one person reduces the availability for others.
  33. Cost-Benefit Analysis - A process used to evaluate the total expected costs versus the total expected benefits of a project or decision to determine its feasibility or worth.
  34. Human Capital - The economic value of an individual's skills, knowledge, and experience that contribute to their productivity and earnings.
  35. Productivity - The measure of output per unit of input, such as labor or capital, indicating the efficiency of production processes.
  36. Nominal GDP - The total value of all goods and services produced within a country, measured using current prices without adjusting for inflation.
  37. Real GDP - The total value of all goods and services produced within a country, adjusted for inflation to reflect constant prices.
  38. Hyperinflation - An extremely high and typically accelerating rate of inflation, often exceeding 50% per month, leading to a collapse in the value of money.
  39. Deflation - A decrease in the general price level of goods and services, often associated with reduced consumer demand and economic contraction.
  40. Subsidy - Financial assistance provided by the government to support or promote certain economic activities, industries, or products.
  41. Tariff - A tax imposed on imported goods and services, intended to protect domestic industries and generate revenue.
  42. Quota - A limit on the quantity of goods that can be imported or exported during a specific time period.
  43. Monetary Base - The total amount of a country's currency in circulation, including both physical currency and reserves held by banks.
  44. Liquidity - The ease with which an asset can be converted into cash without affecting its market price.
  45. Bank Run - A situation where a large number of depositors withdraw their funds from a bank simultaneously, often due to concerns about the bank's solvency.
  46. Moral Hazard - The risk that one party will take on excessive risk because they do not bear the full consequences of their actions, often due to insurance or other forms of protection.
  47. Asymmetric Information - A situation where one party in a transaction has more or better information than the other party, leading to inefficiencies and potential market failures.
  48. Game Theory - The study of strategic interactions between individuals or firms, where the outcome for each participant depends on the actions of others.
  49. Utility - A measure of the satisfaction or benefit derived from consuming goods and services, used to understand consumer preferences and choices.
