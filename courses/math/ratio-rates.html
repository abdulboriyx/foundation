EXPONENTS

What are exponents?

Exponents, also known as powers or indices, are mathematical notation used to indicate repeated multiplication of a number by itself. An exponent is typically written as a superscript to the right of the base number. For example, in the expression 23 2 is the base number and 3 is the exponent. This expression means "2 raised to the power of 3," which indicates that you should multiply 2 by itself three times:

23 = 2 x 2 x 2 = 8

In this case, 8 is the result of raising 2 to the power of 3.

Types of exponentials:

Positive exponents: Positive exponents are like shortcuts for multiplying a number by itself multiple times. For example, let's take 23. The little number 3 is the exponent. It tells us to take the big number 2 and multiply it by itself 3 times. So, 23 2x2x2, which equals 8.

Here's another one: 52. Again, the little number 2 is the exponent. So, we take the big number 5 and multiply it by itself 2 times. That gives us 52= 5x5, which equals 25.

Zero exponents: Imagine you have a number, let's say 5. Now, if you raise 5 to the power of 0, no matter what the number is, the answer is always 1.

For example, 50 = 1.

It's a bit like saying "how many times do I need to multiply 5 by itself to get 1?" Well, you don't need to multiply at all! So, whenever you see a number raised to the power of 0, you can always say it equals 1. It's a rule in math that makes things work out nicely, even though it might seem a little strange at first.

Fractional exponents: Fractional exponents are like roots of numbers. Imagine you have the number 4. If you see 41/2, it means the "half power" or the "square root" of 4. So, 41/2is asking, "What number can I multiply by itself to get 4?" That number is 2, because 2x2=4. Similarly, if you see 81/3, it means the 'third power' or the 'cube root' of 8. So, 81/3 is asking, "What number can I multiply by itself three times to get 8?" That number is 2, because 2x2x2=8.

Negative exponents: Negative exponents might seem a little tricky at first, but they're actually pretty straightforward once you get the hang of them. Let's see together:

Imagine you have a number, let's say 2. If you see 2-3, it means "take the reciprocal of 2 raised to the power of 3." So, 2-3 is asking, 'What is 1 divided by 2 multiplied by itself three times?'. 1/23, which is 1/2x2x2 ,which equals 1/8. So 2-3 = 1/8

Another example: 3-2. It means "take the reciprocal of 3 raised to the power of 2". So, 3-2 is asking, "What's 1 divided by 3 multiplied by itself two times? That's 1/32, which is 1/3x3, which equals 1/9. So, 3-2 = 1/9.

Rules of using exponents

Multiplying Exponential numbers:

The rule for multiplying exponents is a fundamental concept in mathematics, and it has been understood and utilized for centuries. Its origins can be traced back to ancient civilizations, where scholars and mathematicians observed patterns in numbers and developed rules to describe and manipulate them.

When multiplying terms with the same base, you add their exponents. This rule comes from the fundamental property of exponents, which states that when you multiply two numbers with the same base, you add their exponents because you're essentially combining repeated multiplication.

When we have am * an it means we're multiplying a by itself m times and then multiplying it by itself n more times. This can be represented as a * a * a (m times) * a * a * a (n times).

So, the total number of a's being multiplied together is m + n. This means the product am * an is equivalent to a raised to the power of m + n, which can be written as am+n

Example:

Consider 23 * 24. Both terms have the same base, which is 2. To multiply them, you add their exponents: 2(3+4) = 27. This means you're combining the repeated multiplication of 2 by itself 3 times with the repeated multiplication of 2 by itself 4 times, resulting in 2 multiplied by itself 7 times (2*2*2 + 2*2*2*2 = 27).

Dividing exponentials:

When you divide terms with the same base, you subtract their exponents. This rule is based on the concept that dividing by a number is equivalent to subtracting the number of times it's being divided. The rule for dividing terms with the same base involves subtracting their exponents because it reflects the process of repeated division of the base by itself.

Example:

Consider 5 6 5 3 . Both terms have the same base, which is 5. To divide them, you subtract their exponents: 5 6 5 3 = 5(6-3) = 53.
