DECIMALS

What are decimals?

Decimals are a fundamental way to represent numbers that are less than a whole, providing a precise method to express parts of a whole in an organized manner, almost like a special way of writing fractions. By utilizing a dot or period (.) to separate the whole number part from the fractional part, decimals enable us to grasp and manipulate quantities that aren't necessarily whole numbers. For instance, in the number 0.75, the digit 7 signifies 7 tenths, while the digit 5 denotes 5 hundredths, illustrating the intricate details decimals can convey. Such precision is invaluable, especially in measurements like baking a cake or tallying the cost of items, where accuracy is paramount for success.

Decimal value

A decimal value is a number that combines a whole number part and a part that is less than one. This part less than one is called a fraction. The two parts are separated by a dot, which is called a decimal point.

For example, in the decimal value 3.25: the 3 is the whole number part. The 25 represents 25 hundredths, which is a fraction of one.

So, in total, 3.25 represents three whole units and 25 hundredths of another unit.

How to Find Decimal Places:

To determine the value of each digit in a decimal, you can use the powers of ten:

Tenths 10-1 1/10
hundredths 10-2 1/100
thousandths 10-3 1/1000

For example, let's consider the decimal value 6.375:

The 6 is in the units place, which means 6 whole units.

The 3 is in the tenths place, which means 3×10-1or 3/10

The 7 is in the hundredths place, which means 7x10-2 or 7/100.

The 5 is in the thousandth place, which means 5x10-3 or 5/1000.

So, altogether, 6.375 represents six whole units, 3 tenths, 7 hundredths, and 5 thousandths.

Types of decimals

- Terminating Decimals: These decimals have a finite number of digits after the decimal point. For example, 0.75 is a terminating decimal because it ends after the 5, so we know exactly how many tenths and hundredths are there.

- Non-Terminating Decimals: These decimals have an infinite number of digits after the decimal point. Non-terminating decimals can further be classified into two types:

- Repeating Decimals: These decimals have a repeating pattern of digits after the decimal point. For example, 0.333... has the digit 3 repeating indefinitely, indicating one-third.

- Non-Repeating Decimals: These decimals have no repeating pattern after the decimal point. For instance, π (pi) has an indefinite sequence of digits with no repeating pattern, making it a non-repeating decimal.
