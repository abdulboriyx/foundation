Key Terms in History

  1. Empire - A political unit in which a single sovereign authority governs multiple territories or peoples. Example: The Roman Empire.
  2. Revolution - A significant change in political, social, or economic structures, often involving conflict or upheaval. Example: The French Revolution.
  3. Industrialization - The process of developing industries in a country or region on a large scale. It involves the shift from agrarian societies to those dominated by machine-based
  4. Colonialism - The practice of acquiring full or partial control over another country, occupying it with settlers, and exploiting it economically. Example: British colonization of India.
  5. Imperialism - A policy of extending a country's power and influence through diplomacy or military force. Example: European imperialism in Africa in the 19th century.
  6. Feudalism - A hierarchical system of land ownership and obligations, where land is exchanged for service or labor. Example: Medieval Europe’s feudal system.
  7. Democracy - A system of government in which the citizens exercise power by voting. Example: Ancient Athens
  8. Monarchy - A form of government where a single ruler, such as a king or queen, holds sovereign authority, often hereditary. Example: The British monarchy.
  9. Republic - A form of government where the country is considered a "public matter" and the head of state is an elected or appointed official. Example: The Roman Republic.
  10. Constitution - A set of fundamental principles or established precedents according to which a state or other organization is governed. Example: The U.S. Constitution.
  11. Nationalism - Advocacy for the interests and culture of a particular nation, often to the exclusion of other nations. Example: 19th-century European nationalism.
  12. Civil War - A war between opposing groups within the same country. Example: The American Civil War.
  13. Cold War - The period of geopolitical tension between the Soviet Union and the United States after World War II, from 1947 to 1991.
  14. Totalitarianism - A system of government in which the ruler or ruling party has total control over all aspects of public and private life. Example: Nazi Germany.
  15. Communism - A political theory advocating class war, leading to a society in which all property is publicly owned and each person works and is paid according to their abilities and needs. Example: The Soviet Union
  16. Socialism - A political and economic theory advocating for collective or governmental ownership and administration of the means of production and distribution of goods. Example: Early 20th-century European socialism.
  17. Capitalism - An economic system in which private individuals or businesses own capital goods and production is based on supply and demand. Example: Modern U.S. economy.
  18. Fascism - A far-right, authoritarian ultranationalist political ideology characterized by dictatorial power and suppression of opposition. Example: Mussolini’s Italy.
  19. Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand - A measure of how the quantity demanded of one good responds to a change in the price of another good.
  20. Theocracy - A form of government in which religious leaders control the government, interpreting religious law as the law of the land. Example: Ancient Egypt.
  21. Dictatorship - A form of government where a single person or a small group has absolute power. Example: North Korea under Kim Jong-un.
  22. Diplomacy - The practice of conducting negotiations between representatives of states or groups. Example: United Nations peacekeeping efforts.
  23. Enlightenment - An intellectual and philosophical movement that dominated Europe during the 17th and 18th centuries, emphasizing reason, individualism, and skepticism of traditional doctrines. Example: The works of Voltaire and John Locke.
  24. Humanism - A Renaissance intellectual movement that focused on human potential and achievements, emphasizing classical learning. Example: Renaissance scholars studying ancient Greek texts
  25. Absolutism - A political doctrine and practice where unlimited, centralized authority is vested in a monarch or dictator. Example: Louis XIV of France.
  26. Renaissance - A cultural movement between the 14th and 17th centuries in Europe that saw a revival of classical art, literature, and learning. Example: Leonardo da Vinci's works.
  27. Great Depression - A severe worldwide economic depression during the 1930s, marked by high unemployment and financial collapse. Example: The 1929 stock market crash in the U.S.
  28. Human Rights - Rights that are believed to belong justifiably to every person, such as freedom of speech, religion, and equality. Example: The Universal Declaration of Human Rights.
  29. Habeas Corpus - A legal principle that protects individuals from being detained without being charged with a specific crime. Example: Habeas corpus was suspended during the American Civil War.
  30. Protestant Reformation - A 16th-century religious movement that sought to reform the Roman Catholic Church and led to the creation of Protestantism. Example: Martin Luther's 95 Theses.
  31. Laissez-Faire - An economic theory advocating minimal government intervention in the market. Example: Economic policies during the Industrial Revolution.
  32. Coup d'état - The sudden, illegal overthrow of an existing government by a small group. Example: The 1973 military coup in Chile.
  33. Holocaust - The genocide of approximately six million Jews and millions of others during World War II by Nazi Germany. Example: Auschwitz concentration camp.
  34. Decolonization - The process of a colony gaining independence from a colonial power. Example: India's independence from British rule in 1947.
  35. Apartheid - A policy or system of segregation or discrimination on grounds of race. Example: South Africa from 1948 to 1994.
  36. Suffrage - The right to vote in political elections. Example: Women's suffrage movements in the early 20th century
  37. Feudal System - A social and economic system in medieval Europe where land was granted in exchange for military service or labor. Example: The relationship between lords and vassals in medieval England.
  38. Marshall Plan - An American initiative passed in 1948 to aid Western Europe, in which the U.S. provided over $12 billion to help rebuild European economies after World War II.
  39. Manifest Destiny - The 19th-century belief that the expansion of the U.S. across the American continent was justified and inevitable. Example: The annexation of Texas.
  40. Mercantilism - An economic theory that trade generates wealth and is stimulated by the accumulation of profitable balances, which a government should encourage. Example: European colonial powers establishing monopolies.
  41. Age of Exploration - The period during which European powers explored the world by sea in search of new trading routes, wealth, and knowledge. Example: Christopher Columbus' voyages to the Americas.
  42. Guilds - Associations of artisans or merchants who controlled the practice of their craft in a particular town. Example: Medieval guilds in Europe regulating trade.
  43. Neolithic Revolution - The transition of human societies from hunting and gathering to agriculture and settlement. Example: Early farming communities in Mesopotamia.
  44. Pax Romana - A period of peace and stability throughout the Roman Empire, lasting approximately 200 years, starting with the reign of Augustus.
  45. Suffraegette - A woman seeking the right to vote through organized protest. Example: Emmeline Pankhurst in the British suffrage movement.
  46. Ziggurat - A rectangular stepped tower, sometimes surmounted by a temple, in ancient Mesopotamia. Example: The Ziggurat of Ur.
  47. Renaissance Man - A person with many talents or areas of knowledge, inspired by Renaissance ideals. Example: Leonardo da Vinci.
  48. Trench Warfare - A type of combat in which opposing troops fight from trenches facing each other. Example: World War I Western Front.
  49. Treaty of Versailles - The peace treaty that ended World War I and imposed heavy reparations and territorial losses on Germany.
  50. Caste System - A social stratification system in India dividing people into hereditary groups. Example: India's traditional social hierarchy.
  51. Crusades - Religious wars sanctioned by the Latin Church in the medieval period, primarily to recover the Holy Land from Muslim rule. Example: The First Crusade (1096-1099).
