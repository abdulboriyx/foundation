DECIMALS

What are decimals?

Decimals are a way of representing numbers that are not whole numbers. They use a dot (called a decimal point) to separate the whole part of the number from the fractional part. Whole Numbers and Decimal Point: Whole numbers are to the left of the decimal point. Fractional part: Numbers to the right of the decimal point represent parts of a whole.

Types of decimals

Decimals can be classified into different types based on their characteristics. Here are the main types of decimals, explained simply with examples:

  1. Terminating decimals: A terminating decimal has a finite number of digits after the decimal point. It ends, or terminates, after a certain number of decimal places.

    2. Examples:

    • 0.5: This is a terminating decimal because it has just one digit after the decimal point.
    • 3.14: This is a terminating decimal because it has two digits after the decimal point and then stops.
  2. Repeating decimals: A repeating decimal has one or more digits that repeat infinitely. These repeating digits can start immediately after the decimal point or after a few digits.

    3. Examples:

    • 0.3333....: This is a repeating decimal where the digit 3 repeats indefinitely. It can be written as 0.3
    • 1.6666...: This is a repeating decimal where the digit 6 repeats indefinitely. It can be written as 1.6
    • 0.123123123...: This is a repeating decimal where the sequence 123 repeats indefinitely. It can be written as 0.123
  3. Non-terminating, non-repeating decimals: These decimals go on forever without repeating a pattern. Such numbers are also called irrational numbers because they cannot be expressed as a simple fraction.

    4. Examples:

    • π (pi) = 3.141592653...: The decimal expansion of pi goes on forever without repeating.
    • √2 = 1.41421356...: The square root of 2 is a non-terminating, non-repeating decimal.

Whole Number Part

  1. Units (Ones) The digit immediately to the left of the decimal point. Represents whole numbers from 0 to 9.
    • In 45.678, the digit 5 is in the units place, representing 5 whole units.
  2. Tenths: The second digit to the left of the decimal point. Represents multiples of ten.
    • In 345.678, the digit 3 is in the hundreds place, representing 3 × 100 = 300.
  3. Hundredths: The third digit to the left of the decimal point. Represents multiples of one hundred.
    • In 345.678, the digit 3 is in the hundreds place, representing 3 × 100 = 300.
  4. Thousands The fourth digit to the left of the decimal point. Represents multiples of one thousand.
    • In 2345.678, the digit 2 is in the thousands place, representing 2 × 1000 = 2000

Decimal (Fractional) Part

  1. Tenths: The first digit to the right of the decimal point. Represents one-tenth (1/10) of a whole number
    • In 45.678, the digit 6 is in the tenths place, representing 6 x 10 = 0.6
  2. Hundredths: The second digit to the right of the decimal point. Represents one-hundredth ( 1/100) of a whole number.
    • In 45.678, the digit 7 is in the hundredths place, representing 7 x 1/100 = 0.07
  3. Thousandths: The third digit to the right of the decimal point. Represents one-thousandth ( 1/ 1000 ) of a whole number.
    • In 45.678, the digit 8 is in the thousandths place, representing 8 x 1/1000 = 0.008

Multiplication

Multiplication of decimals involves multiplying two decimal numbers to get a product. When multiplying decimals, the decimal points in the numbers being multiplied do not need to be aligned, but the total number of decimal places in the product is determined by the sum of the decimal places in the numbers being multiplied.
