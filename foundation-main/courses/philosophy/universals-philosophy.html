Universals

What are universals in metaphysics?

Universals are abstract entities or concepts that are characterized by their ability to be instantiated or exemplified by multiple particular objects or entities. They represent qualities, properties, or relations that can be shared among different instances without being confined to any one particular object or occurrence. Universals are often contrasted with particulars, which are specific instances or individual entities that embody these universal properties in concrete form. In philosophical terms, universals are considered to be ontologically distinct from particulars. They are understood as entities that exist in a different manner than concrete objects in the physical world. While particulars are singular and located in space and time, universals are conceptual entities that exist independently of specific instances. They are often regarded as timeless and transcendent, existing beyond the physical manifestations of the world.
Consider the universal concept of "roundness". Roundness is a universal property that describes the shape of a circle. It is an abstract quality that characterizes circles and other objects that share this shape.
A ball: While each ball is a particular entity (with its own unique properties and history), the round shape it possesses is an instantiation of the universal concept of roundness. The ball shares this property with other round objects like wheels, coins, or planets.

Why are universals essential?

Concept Formation and Communication: Universals provide the basis for forming concepts and categories in language and thought. They allow us to group together diverse particular instances that share common characteristics. For example, the universal concept of "chair" encompasses various specific chairs we encounter, enabling us to recognize and communicate about chairs without having to specify each individual instance.

Knowledge and Generalization:Universals facilitate knowledge acquisition and generalization. By abstracting away from individual particulars, we can identify patterns, regularities, and laws that apply universally. For instance, the scientific understanding of gravity involves recognizing universal principles that govern the behavior of objects, regardless of their specific identities.

Prediction and Explanation: Universals help in predicting outcomes and explaining phenomena across different contexts. When we understand universal principles or laws (e.g., laws of physics), we can predict how things will behave under different conditions and explain why certain events occur. This predictive and explanatory power is crucial for scientific inquiry and understanding causal relationships.

Language and Thought: Universals are fundamental to language and thought processes. They provide the semantic content that allows us to communicate effectively by referring to abstract qualities or properties. For instance, words like "beauty," "justice," or "freedom" refer to universal concepts that people can understand and discuss despite variations in individual experiences or interpretations.

Types of Universals

Concrete Particulars

Natural universals encompass qualities that are inherent in physical objects and phenomena. These universals describe attributes that multiple objects can possess simultaneously, illustrating their fundamental role in defining the characteristics of entities in the physical world. Examples of natural universals include:

  1. Color: Universals such as redness, blueness, and greenness are qualities that various objects can exhibit, regardless of their specific identity.
  2. Shape: Universals like circularity, triangularity, and rectangularity describe geometric forms that can be instantiated by different objects.
  3. Size: Concepts such as largeness, smallness, and dimensions are universals that quantify the physical extent of objects.
  4. Material Properties: Universals like hardness, softness, density, and elasticity describe physical properties that objects can share.

Abstract Universals

Abstract universals transcend physical instantiation and include concepts, principles, and ideas that are crucial for understanding abstract reasoning and conceptual frameworks. Examples of abstract universals include:

  1. Mathematical Concepts: Universals such as numbers (e.g., integers, fractions) and mathematical operations (e.g., addition, subtraction) are essential for mathematical reasoning.
  2. Logical Principles: Universals such as truth, validity, and logical necessity are fundamental to logical reasoning and argumentation.
  3. Conceptual Categories: Universals like existence, identity, and essence are foundational in philosophical debates about the nature of reality and being.

Metaphysical Universals

Metaphysical universals explore fundamental aspects of existence itself, including the nature of substances, identities, and the basic properties that define entities beyond their observable qualities. Examples of metaphysical universals include:

  1. Substance: The universal concept of substance refers to the underlying essence or nature of entities that persist through change.
  2. Identity: Universals such as self-identity and persistence over time address how entities maintain continuity despite undergoing transformations.
  3. Essential Properties: Universals like beingness (the quality of existence itself) and individuation (what makes one entity distinct from another) are central to understanding the essence of entities.

Relational Universals

Relational universals encompass the various types of relationships that can exist between objects, entities, or concepts. These universals highlight the interconnectedness and dependency of entities on one another. Examples include:

  1. Spatial Relations: Universals such as distance, direction (north, south, etc.), and adjacency describe spatial relationships between objects.
  2. Temporal Relations: Universals like simultaneity, succession, and duration describe temporal relationships between events or moments.
  3. Social Relations: Universals such as friendship, authority, and kinship describe interpersonal relationships and social dynamics among individuals.

Aestethic and Moral Universals

Aesthetic and moral universals capture qualities that are recognized across different cultures and contexts, illustrating their universal appeal and significance in human experience. Examples include:

  1. Aesthetic Universals: Qualities like beauty, harmony, and symmetry are recognized as aesthetic universals that transcend cultural boundaries.
  2. Moral Universals: Concepts such as justice, fairness, and compassion are considered moral universals that guide ethical reasoning and behavior across diverse societies.

Theories of universals

Realism Theory

Realism posits that universals exist independently of human thought and are objective entities that exist whether or not they are instantiated by particular objects. Realists argue that universals have a mind-independent existence and that they are the ontological basis for the similarities observed among different particulars. Realism can be further divided into several sub-theories:

  1. Platonic Realism: According to Plato, universals exist in a separate realm of abstract forms or ideas that are more real and immutable than the physical world. Particulars in the physical world are imperfect copies or instances of these perfect forms.
  2. Aristotelian Realism: Aristotle's view emphasizes that universals exist within particulars as their essences or forms. Universals are instantiated by particulars and provide the basis for their classification and identity.


Consider a red apple. According to substratum theory, the apple has an underlying substratum that supports its properties like color (redness), shape (roundness), and texture (smoothness). The substratum itself is not red, round, or smooth, but it is what makes the apple a cohesive universal with these properties.

Nominalism

Nominalism denies the existence of universals as abstract entities independent of particular objects. Instead, nominalists argue that universals are merely names or labels applied to groups of similar objects based on shared attributes. Nominalism asserts that only particulars exist, and what we call universals are just convenient linguistic conventions used to categorize and describe similarities among particulars.

Conceptualism

Conceptualism occupies a middle ground between realism and nominalism. It holds that universals exist, but only as concepts or mental constructs within human cognition. According to conceptualists, universals are not mind-independent entities like in realism but are nonetheless real insofar as they exist as conceptual tools used by human minds to understand the world.

Historically Notable Works on Universals

  1. - Plato's Dialogues (e.g., "Parmenides", "Phaedo", "Republic"): Plato's dialogues explore the realm of Forms or Ideas, where universals exist as perfect, immutable entities separate from the physical world. Universals, such as Beauty, Justice, and the Good, are eternal and serve as the ideal forms that particular objects imperfectly instantiate.
  2. - Aristotle's "Metaphysics": Aristotle's "Metaphysics" addresses universals within the context of his theory of substance and essence. Aristotle distinguishes between universals (e.g., humanity, redness) and substances (particular instances of those universals). He argues that universals exist in particulars as their forms or essences, shaping their identity and classification.
  3. - William of Ockham's "Summa Logicae": William of Ockham, a prominent medieval philosopher and theologian, is known for his nominalist stance on universals. In "Summa Logicae," he argues against the existence of universals as real entities, asserting that only particular objects exist, and universals are merely mental constructs or linguistic conventions.
  4. - John Locke's "Essay Concerning Human Understanding": Locke's work explores the nature of knowledge and perception, including his theory of ideas. He distinguishes between primary qualities (inherent in objects) and secondary qualities (dependent on perception). While Locke acknowledges the use of general terms (universals), he tends towards a conceptualist view, suggesting that universals are mental constructs based on similarities perceived among particulars.

Challenges & Problems of Universals

Problem of Individuation and Multiplicity:

One significant challenge to universals is the problem of individuation — the question of what distinguishes one particular from another if they share identical universal properties. For example, two red apples may possess the universal property of "redness," but what makes them distinct entities? Critics argue that if universals are the sole basis for identity, it becomes difficult to explain the uniqueness and individuation of particular objects. This challenge is particularly acute in cases where multiple objects instantiate the same universal properties without any distinguishing features.

Problem of Abstract Entities

Critics argue against the existence of universals as abstract entities that exist independently of particular instances. Nominalists, for instance, contend that universals are merely linguistic or mental constructs used to classify and categorize particular objects based on shared characteristics. They argue that there is no need to posit the existence of universals as separate entities beyond individual instances because all we encounter are particulars with specific properties.

Problem of Causal Efficiacy

Another critique concerns the causal efficacy of universals. Critics question whether universals, as abstract entities or properties, can have any causal impact on the physical world. For example, how does the universal property of "redness" cause an object to appear red or affect its interactions with other objects? Critics argue that universals lack causal powers because they are conceptual or linguistic constructs rather than concrete entities capable of exerting influence in the physical realm.
