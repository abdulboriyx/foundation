Metaethics

What are metaethics?

Metaethics is a branch of philosophy that examines the nature of ethics and moral judgments. It explores fundamental questions like whether moral truths are objective or subjective. Those who believe in moral realism argue that there are objective moral facts independent of human beliefs, while moral anti-realists see morality as subjective, shaped by personal or cultural views.

Another focus in metaethics is on cognitive versus non-cognitive theories of ethics. Cognitive theories suggest moral judgments express beliefs that can be true or false, while non-cognitive theories propose they express attitudes or emotions rather than factual claims.

Additionally, metaethics considers ethical naturalism versus non-naturalism. Ethical naturalists think moral properties can be understood through empirical investigation and are based on natural properties, like human well-being. Non-naturalists argue moral truths are distinct from natural facts and known through intuition rather than empirical study.

Why are metaethics ethics essential?

- Understanding the Foundations of Ethics: Metaethics delves into the fundamental questions about the nature, origin, and justification of ethical principles. For instance, by studying metaethical theories like moral realism and moral anti-realism, one gains insight into whether ethical truths are objective facts or subjective beliefs shaped by culture and individual perspectives. This understanding provides a solid foundation for grappling with complex moral dilemmas and forming coherent ethical frameworks.

- Clarifying Ethical Disagreements: Metaethics helps clarify the roots of ethical disagreements. By understanding these underlying metaethical positions, individuals can better articulate their ethical perspectives and engage in constructive dialogue with others.

- Enhancing Ethical Reasoning: Studying metaethics sharpens one's ability to critically evaluate ethical arguments. For instance, cognitive theories in metaethics suggest that moral judgments express beliefs that can be assessed for truth or falsity, while non-cognitive theories argue they express emotions or attitudes. This distinction enables individuals to analyze ethical claims rigorously and discern the logical coherence of moral arguments.

Theories of Metaethics

Moral Realism

Moral realism posits that there are objective moral facts that exist independently of human beliefs and attitudes. These moral truths are akin to truths in science or mathematics, and they provide a framework for understanding right and wrong universally. For example, the statement "It is wrong to torture innocent children for fun" is considered objectively true under moral realism because it aligns with moral facts that exist independently of individual opinions or cultural norms.

Moral Relativism

Moral relativism argues that moral judgments are relative to particular cultures, societies, or individuals. This theory holds that there are no universal moral truths, and what is considered morally right or wrong can vary based on cultural beliefs and practices. For instance, practices such as polygamy might be morally acceptable in certain cultures but condemned in others. According to moral relativism, the moral status of actions is contingent upon the norms and values of the society in which they occur.

Cognitive vs Non-Cognitive Theories

Metaethics also considers how we understand moral language and judgments. Cognitive theories, such as ethical naturalism or ethical realism, argue that moral judgments express propositions that can be true or false. For example, the statement "Lying is morally wrong" expresses a belief that can be evaluated for its truthfulness. Non-cognitive theories, on the other hand, propose that moral judgments do not aim to describe facts but rather express emotions, attitudes, or commands. For instance, saying "Lying is wrong" might express disapproval or a command not to lie rather than stating a fact about lying's moral status.

Contructivism

Constructivism in metaethics suggests that moral truths are constructed by rational agents who aim to find solutions to practical problems of cooperation. It emphasizes the role of reason and deliberation in establishing moral norms that are binding on all rational beings. For example, constructivists might argue that principles like reciprocity and fairness emerge from rational agents seeking to resolve conflicts of interest in a justifiable way.

Historically Notable Works on Metaethics

  1. -"Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals" by Immanuel Kant (1785): Kant's "Groundwork" is a foundational work in modern metaethics, particularly in deontological ethics. In this text, Kant introduces the concept of the categorical imperative, a universal moral principle that guides ethical decision-making. The categorical imperative asserts that actions are morally right if they can be willed as a universal law without contradiction. For example, lying is morally wrong according to Kant because if lying were universalized, it would undermine trust and communication essential for moral interaction. Kant's work laid the groundwork for understanding ethics as grounded in rationality and the concept of duty.
  2. - "Ethics" by G.E. Moore (1912): G.E. Moore's "Ethics" is notable for its defense of ethical intuitionism, a metaethical theory that asserts that ethical truths can be known through intuition or immediate apprehension. Moore famously presented the "open-question argument" against ethical naturalism, arguing that ethical properties (like 'goodness') cannot be reduced to natural properties (like 'pleasure'). His work influenced the development of non-naturalism in metaethics, challenging the prevailing views of ethical naturalism and emotivism prevalent at the time.
  3. - "Language, Truth, and Logic" by A.J. Ayer (1936): A.J. Ayer's "Language, Truth, and Logic" is a seminal work in the logical positivist movement, which had a significant impact on metaethics. Ayer argued for emotivism, a non-cognitive metaethical theory that asserts ethical statements express emotions, attitudes, or commands rather than propositions that can be true or false. For example, according to Ayer, saying "Stealing is wrong" expresses disapproval of stealing rather than stating a fact about its moral status. Ayer's work contributed to the development of non-cognitivist theories in metaethics, challenging the traditional cognitive approaches to ethics.
  4. - "Ethics" by Alasdair MacIntyre (1981): Alasdair MacIntyre's "Ethics" is notable for its critique of modern moral philosophy and its advocacy for virtue ethics. MacIntyre argues for a return to Aristotelian ethics, emphasizing the importance of virtues, narratives, and social practices in ethical life. He critiques the fragmentation of moral discourse in modernity and calls for a renewal of moral traditions grounded in communal practices and virtues. MacIntyre's work revitalized interest in virtue ethics within contemporary metaethics and contributed to ongoing debates about the role of tradition and community in ethical life.

Obstacles and Concerns in Metaethics

Metaethical Disagreements

Metaethics explores the nature, justification, and meaning of ethical principles and judgments. Disagreements in metaethics can impact metaethics ethics by challenging the foundations of ethical theories themselves. For instance, debates over whether moral truths are objective or subjective (moral realism vs. moral relativism) can influence how metaethics ethical principles are formulated and applied.

Metaethical disagreements can create uncertainty about the validity and reliability of metaethics ethical theories. If foundational metaethics questions remain unresolved, it becomes challenging to establish robust metaethics principles that are widely accepted or applicable across different contexts.

The Is-Ought Gap

The "is-ought" problem, famously articulated by David Hume, poses a fundamental challenge in metaethics. Hume argued that ethical statements ("ought" statements) cannot be logically derived from purely descriptive statements about the world ("is" statements). This means that factual claims about how things are (descriptive statements) do not automatically imply moral claims about how things should be (normative statements). For example, observing that a particular action is likely to cause harm (an "is" statement) does not necessarily lead to a conclusion about whether one ought to refrain from that action (an "ought" statement). Bridging this gap between descriptive facts and prescriptive moral judgments remains a central concern in metaethics.

Cognitive vs. Non-Cognitive Theories

Cognitive vs. Non-Cognitive Theories: Metaethics involves a distinction between cognitive and non-cognitive theories of ethics, which reflects debates about the nature of moral statements and their truth values.

Cognitive Theories: Cognitive theories (such as moral realism and some forms of ethical naturalism) assert that ethical statements express propositions that can be true or false. These theories typically aim to provide an objective basis for moral judgments, grounded in facts about the world or moral principles.

Non-Cognitive Theories: Non-cognitive theories (such as emotivism and some forms of expressivism) argue that ethical statements do not express propositions with truth values but instead convey emotions, attitudes, or prescriptions. According to non-cognitivism, moral judgments are expressions of approval, disapproval, or commands rather than statements about objective facts.
